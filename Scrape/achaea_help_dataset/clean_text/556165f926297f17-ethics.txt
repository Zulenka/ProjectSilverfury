7.30.1 AI Ethics
Statement on
AI
Ethics
regarding Cognitive AI
We understand that some people may have an
issue
with generative AI, whether that's because of some alleged ethical violations with what data was used to
train
them, or simply objecting to AI doing what may be considered rightful human work by some.
Regarding the human work aspect,
nothing
about our cognitive AI takes jobs away from anyone. It's not possible for a human to craft AI responses quickly enough to be even remotely feasible, and while people can possess
denizens
in the game and have them give human-written responses, these take
time
and aren't scalable. The cost to staff a
system
24/7 that had people ready to respond asap to
anything
that
triggers
a cognitive
denizen
would be so high that the system would never get used. It would also, aside from being very expensive, be -much- slower in terms of response time.
The only way cognitive denizens are possible is with AI - they don't replace humans creators in any way, but they do augment the
experience
for human players.
Regarding the
training
of AI models, while this isn't the place for a nuanced discussion of AI ethics, we recognize there's a range of valid and principled viewpoints people have here. Our
point
of view is evolving as AI technology evolves, but right now we find that the benefits of experimenting with this new technology are worth it while the legal
status
of using the data they are trained on is litigated and potentially regulated. We'll be watching those developments closely, and will, of course, follow American law there. We built this system in such a way as to relatively easily accommodate, at least in theory, switching to
other
AI
systems
, so if we
learn
that the way a particular AI model was trained is now illegal, or that a new AI model that performs at least as well has less controversy over its training data, we'll switch to a new one.
Matt Mihaly
CEO, Iron Realms Entertainment
